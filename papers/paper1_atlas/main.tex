\begin{abstract}
Oceanic mass redistribution produces direct gravitational attraction, elastic
loading responses, and gravity gradients over timescales from seconds to months.
We develop a common process--distance--instrument framework that evaluates these
signals using explicit source geometry, observation duration, spectral coverage,
and instrument-specific noise. The framework distinguishes tides, storm surge,

\section{Introduction}
Environmental Newtonian-noise studies already quantify atmospheric, seismic,
and ocean-related gravitational disturbances for gravitational-wave facilities;
they are not instrument-noise calculations developed here. Likewise, terrestrial
gravimetry has detected non-tidal ocean loading during storm surges. The present
increment is a cross-process, cross-distance, cross-instrument ocean-sensing atlas
under one auditable convention. The atlas asks where a process is detectable,
boundary-detectable, outside available frequency coverage, or physically
undetectable under stated assumptions.

\section{Methods}
\subsection{Observables and sign conventions}
All kernels use SI units. For a surface mass anomaly $\Delta\sigma$,
\begin{equation}
 \Delta\mathbf{g}_{\mathrm{direct}}(\mathbf{r},t)
 =G\int_A \Delta\sigma(\mathbf{x},t)
 \frac{\mathbf{x}-\mathbf{r}}{|\mathbf{x}-\mathbf{r}|^3}\,\mathrm{d}A.
\end{equation}
For a volume-density anomaly $\rho'$, the analogous integral is evaluated over
volume. The gravity-gradient convention is
$T_{ij}=\partial g_i/\partial x_{\mathrm{observation},j}$. Direct attraction,
deformation gravity, internal-mass/potential response, and vertical displacement
are stored separately.

\subsection{Earth and grid geometry}
Reference calculations include local planar, mean-radius spherical, and WGS~84
ellipsoidal geometry. Spherical and ellipsoidal cell areas are integrated in
their native surface coordinates. Missing ocean values, hard masks, and partial
coastal-cell fractions are explicit. Grid refinement and domain truncation are
tested before a dataset is admitted to a result.

\subsection{Spectra and detectability}
For transient signals, expected matched-filter signal-to-noise ratio is
\begin{equation}
 \rho^2=4\int_{f_{\min}}^{f_{\max}}
 \frac{|\tilde{s}(f)|^2}{S_n(f)}\,\mathrm{d}f.
\end{equation}
Fourier normalization, detrending, window gain, one-sided PSD, and observation
duration are versioned. A classification is forbidden unless the instrument
curve covers at least the predeclared fraction of signal spectral energy.
Vertical gravity, gravity gradient, and gravitational-wave equivalent strain
remain different observables and are never merged into one sensitivity curve.

\subsection{Parameter design and uncertainty}
Latin-hypercube designs are labelled as scenario envelopes unless every
dimension has a source-supported probability distribution. Engineering ranges
cannot be reported as probabilistic 5--95\% intervals. Density-compensated
models retain cancellation and far-field suppression rather than replacing them
with equivalent uncompensated water columns.

\section{Discussion}
The principal interpretation will be the boundary between detectable,
coverage-limited, and physically suppressed signals. In particular, compensated
internal structures can decay much faster than surface-load estimates, while
long-period processes may be strong but unclassifiable when empirical noise
curves do not cover their frequency band.
