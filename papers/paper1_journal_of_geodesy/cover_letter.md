Dear Editor,

Please consider “Frequency-Support Requirements for Assessing Direct
Ocean-Induced Gravity Signals in Terrestrial Gravimetry” for publication in
*Journal of Geodesy*.

The manuscript addresses a recurring evidential problem in terrestrial
gravimetry: a direct-gravity signal amplitude cannot be converted into a
detectability statement when the published noise evidence does not cover the
signal's frequency support. We apply one component-resolved convention to six
representative ocean-process families and 1,446 finite records. None reaches a
fixed 90% unweighted spectral-energy coverage threshold within three reviewed
vertical-gravity reference bands, so no SNR-based detectable or undetectable
classification is assigned.

The revision adds a preregistered temporal and spectral convergence audit. Dense
boundary-aware integration and finer analytic-source cadence leave the
0-of-1,446 result unchanged. Exact-cycle tide and internal-wave estimates are
stable, while eddy, storm-surge, tsunami and landslide lower edges are shown to
depend on the finite record window. We report that dependence directly rather
than presenting the values as universal process or instrument-design constants.
The software also reproduces 50 published LoadDef benchmark rows, and an open
Helgoland model calculation provides a component-resolved model-scale
consistency check against a published elastic gravity-to-height ratio.

The manuscript distinguishes direct attraction, elastic/potential response,
station-height response and gravity gradients. It does not claim a complete
detectability atlas, universal non-detectability, or a total terrestrial
observable for all six process families. The named authors have approved the
submission and contribution statement. Code, compact metrics, figure inputs and
the fixed analysis protocols are archived at the DOI stated in the manuscript;
restricted and bulk source data are represented by provenance manifests rather
than redistributed.

This manuscript is original, is not under consideration elsewhere, and has
been approved by all authors.
