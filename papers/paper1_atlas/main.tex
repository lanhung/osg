\begin{abstract}
Oceanic mass redistribution produces direct gravitational attraction and, for
surface loads, elastic/potential and height responses over timescales from
seconds to months. We develop an evidence-bounded process--distance--instrument
framework with explicit source geometry, observation duration, spectral
coverage, and instrument-specific noise. The primary six-process production
ensemble contains 1,446 records of direct radial gravity; elastic and height
components are retained separately where evaluated and are not implied for
every process branch. None passes the fixed 90\% signal-energy
coverage gate for the three reviewed vertical-gravity literature references: tide,
storm surge, eddy and internal-wave signals have no overlapping band, whereas
tsunami and landslide records have only partial coverage. We therefore assign
no
SNR classifications rather than extrapolating instrument curves. A separate
open-data Helgoland calculation reproduces the published BSH gravity-to-height
ratio within 15.6\%. A temporal/spectral audit confirms that the 0-of-1,446
result is unchanged by dense boundary-aware frequency integration or finer
analytic-source cadence. Exact-cycle tide and internal-wave lower edges are
stable near $2\times10^{-5}$~Hz, whereas eddy, storm-surge, tsunami and
landslide values vary with the finite record window; all tested values remain
below the most permissive reviewed $5\times10^{-4}$~Hz curve edge. The
framework therefore identifies a robust common-support deficit without
promoting finite-record quantities to universal process constants.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
Ocean tides, non-tidal ocean loading and storm surges already contribute to
terrestrial gravity and displacement observations~\cite{Boy2003,BoyLyard2008,
Voigt2024,Zhang2026}. Superconducting and atom-interferometric gravimeters reach
high precision, but their sensitivity, stability, self-noise and site noise are
different quantities with different frequency support~\cite{Banka1999,
Rosat2011,Schafer2020,Menoret2018,Peters2001}.

Environmental Newtonian-noise studies provide the broader theory of gravity
fluctuations from moving density fields~\cite{Harms2015,VanCamp2017}. Work for
gravitational-wave facilities often reports strain-equivalent noise from
seismic or atmospheric sources~\cite{Creighton2008,Brundu2022,Hall2022}; those
results are not vertical-gravimeter noise curves. Long terrestrial records also
require removal of tides and other background components, and different
procedures can leave residuals or distort target signals~\cite{Valencio2017}.

Simple comparison of a source peak amplitude with one quoted sensitivity value
therefore cannot establish detectability. The source spectrum must overlap a
measured noise spectrum, the observable and units must agree, and enough signal
energy must lie inside the reported band before an SNR is considered interpretable.

Here we ask that coverage question under one auditable convention for six
representative process families. The contribution is a cross-process direct-
radial-gravity framework, an explicit coverage gate, quantitative lower-band-
edge requirements, mass-compensation tests and an open Helgoland model
validation. The gravity kernels, elastic loading theory and instrument concepts
are prior work, and no universal detectable/undetectable classification is
claimed.

\section{Related physical observables}
Direct attraction is the Newtonian acceleration at a specified observation
point. A surface load also deforms the Earth and changes its potential; a
terrestrial gravimeter can additionally respond to station-height change. In
general these contributions must be combined only after their reference frame,
sign and Green-function conventions have been validated~\cite{Farrell1972,
DziewonskiAnderson1981,Martens2019}. The six-process production ensemble in this
paper contains direct radial gravity, not that total terrestrial observable.

Gravity gradient $T_{ij}=\partial g_i/\partial x_j$ has units of s$^{-2}$ and a
different instrument class~\cite{McGuirk2002,Sorrentino2014}. Gravitational-wave
equivalent strain is another derived observable tied to detector geometry and a
transfer function. Gradient and strain foundations are retained as supplemental
context; neither is compared with the vertical-gravity curves used here.

Figure~\ref{fig:framework} fixes the observable boundary used throughout the
paper. It is a provenance diagram, not a claim that every process branch has a
validated total-observable calculation.

\section{Methods}
\subsection{Observables and sign conventions}
All kernels use SI units. For a surface mass anomaly $\Delta\sigma$,
\begin{equation}
 \Delta\mathbf{g}_{\mathrm{direct}}(\mathbf{r},t)
 =G\int_A \Delta\sigma(\mathbf{x},t)
 \frac{\mathbf{x}-\mathbf{r}}{|\mathbf{x}-\mathbf{r}|^3}\,\mathrm{d}A.
\end{equation}
For a volume-density anomaly $\rho'$, the analogous integral is evaluated over
volume. The gravity-gradient convention is
$T_{ij}=\partial g_i/\partial x_{\mathrm{observation},j}$. Direct attraction,
combined elastic gravity and vertical displacement are stored separately. The
LoadDef CE gravity term combines deformation and potential response according
to its source convention and is never represented as direct attraction.

\subsection{Earth, grid and coastal geometry}
Reference calculations include local planar, mean-radius spherical, and WGS~84
ellipsoidal geometry. Spherical and ellipsoidal cell areas are integrated in
their native surface coordinates. Missing ocean values, hard masks, and partial
coastal-cell fractions are explicit. Grid refinement and domain truncation are
tested before a dataset is included in a result.

For idealized compact surface anomalies evaluated against terrestrial
instruments, source cells and the station lie on a spherical Earth. Distance is
the great-circle surface standoff from the represented three-standard-deviation
source support. Compact anomalies translate parallel to an idealized coastline,
preventing a source cell from coinciding with the station. A rejected local-plane
near-field calculation is retained in the experiment preregistration history:
its midpoint quadrature exceeded the continuous-sheet amplitude bound and did
not enter the fixed output.

\subsection{Ocean-process models}
The six process families are summarized in Table~\ref{tab:processes}. Surface
and compensated three-dimensional variants are retained only where physically
supported. Engineering fixtures used for software validation are excluded from
scientific uncertainty statements. Parameter values are linked to named events,
catalogue means or published ranges. They define sensitivity designs, not
occurrence probabilities or population percentiles. The mesoscale-eddy Gaussian
branch uses catalogue-scale amplitude, radius and propagation speed from
Chelton et al.~\cite{Chelton2011}; observed energetics and vertical structure
show why SSH does not uniquely specify an interior density anomaly
\cite{Luecke2017,Li2022Eddy,George2021}. Internal-wave scales are anchored to
field and satellite studies~\cite{LiFarmer2011,Huang2016,Zhao2016,Rainville2013},
while tsunami and landslide branches retain named-source provenance
\cite{Tang2012,Lovholt2017,Kim2019}. Surface-wave packets and density dipoles
are mass balanced before gravity integration.

\subsection{Spectra and detectability}
For transient signals, expected matched-filter signal-to-noise ratio is
\begin{equation}
 \rho^2=4\int_{f_{\min}}^{f_{\max}}
 \frac{|\tilde{s}(f)|^2}{S_n(f)}\,\mathrm{d}f.
\end{equation}
Fourier normalization, detrending, one-sided PSD, and observation duration are
versioned. An SNR classification is considered interpretable only if the
instrument curve covers at least 90\% of signal spectral energy. The 90\%
working threshold retains most signal energy while limiting dependence on an
unmeasured band; conclusions are evaluated separately at
50\%, 75\%, 90\% and 95\%; these are coverage fractions, not detection
probabilities. Vertical gravity, gravity gradient, and gravitational-wave
equivalent strain remain different observables and are never merged into one
sensitivity curve. The reviewed vertical-gravity entries are an
iGrav quiet self-noise anchor over $10^{-3}$--$10^{-1}$~Hz and short-term
AQG-A01 and FG5\#228 white-noise anchors over the source-reported
$5\times10^{-4}$--$10^{-2}$~Hz plateau; their intervals are not extrapolated.

For a finite, constant-detrended signal, the coverage quantity is
\begin{equation}
 C(f_{\mathrm{low}},f_{\mathrm{high}})=
 \frac{\int_{f_{\mathrm{low}}}^{f_{\mathrm{high}}}|\tilde{s}(f)|^2\,\mathrm{d}f}
 {\int_{0^+}^{f_{\mathrm{Nyquist}}}|\tilde{s}(f)|^2\,\mathrm{d}f}.
\end{equation}
This criterion deliberately uses unweighted signal energy, not accumulated
SNR. It is an evidence-admissibility check applied before the matched-filter
calculation, not an
estimate of the fraction of detectable SNR in the reviewed band. The fixed
primary convention uses a rectangular window and trapezoidal integration of
positive-frequency Fourier amplitudes. Trapezoidal integration requires at
least two frequency samples in a common interval; a one-bin interval is
reported as numerically unresolved rather than assigned zero physical energy.
This implementation condition is separate from the 90\% criterion. An
independent review
audit also applies rectangular and periodic Hann window one-sided periodogram
bin sums. The maximum admissible lower edge is the highest resolved frequency
for which $C$ reaches the requested fraction at a fixed upper edge.

A separate temporal/spectral convergence audit samples the same finite-record
transform at 1, 4, 16 and 64 times the native Fourier density, compares
regenerable analytic signals at 1, 2, 4 and 8 times their baseline cadence, and
varies physically defined record windows. Zero padding only samples the
finite-record DTFT more densely and creates no new temporal information. Grid
and cadence medians must change by less than 5\% at the final two resolutions.
The largest two record windows use a separate 10\% stability gate. Hourly BSH
storm fields are never interpolated to manufacture higher-frequency support.

\subsection{Parameter design and uncertainty}
Latin-hypercube and stratified designs are labelled as scenario envelopes unless
every dimension has a source-supported probability distribution. Engineering
ranges cannot be reported as probabilistic 5--95\% intervals. Density-compensated
models retain cancellation and far-field suppression rather than replacing them
with equivalent uncompensated water columns. Source standoffs are 10, 30, 100,
300 and 1,000~km, and every coverage-limited outcome is retained.

\section{Validation}
Analytic and regression tests cover point masses, finite disks, rectangles,
Gaussian sheets,
volume cells, gradient symmetry and trace, WGS~84 area, mass conservation, grid
convergence, domain truncation, and time/frequency normalization. Test counts and
software-behaviour details are reported in the reproducibility supplement; the
scientific validation below rests on analytic errors, published benchmarks and
the independent Helgoland case rather than the number of tests.

The LoadDef v1.2.2 PREM/CE provider reproduces all 50 published benchmark rows
used by the project for angular distance, radial displacement and combined
elastic gravity~\cite{Martens2019}. Direct attraction is calculated independently;
LoadDef's Newtonian term is not added a second time.

The Helgoland model-only reproduction uses 242 BSH-HBMnoku files and 720 hourly
fields conservatively remapped to a 1/8-degree WGS~84 grid. Direct attraction,
combined elastic gravity and vertical displacement remain separate. After the
fixed sign conversion at the literature-comparison boundary, the computed
gravity-to-height ratio is $-2.266$~nm~s$^{-2}$~mm$^{-1}$, compared with
$-2.684$~nm~s$^{-2}$~mm$^{-1}$ reported by Voigt et
al.~\cite{Voigt2024}. The 15.6\% difference passes the 20\% protocol tolerance
fixed before the production result was accepted. The tolerance accommodates
the documented replacement of SPOTL/Gutenberg--Bullen and a three-year tidal
analysis by LoadDef PREM/CE and a fixed 30-day harmonic fit; it is an engineering
consistency tolerance, not a statistical uncertainty interval.

The version-controlled component audit quantifies, but does not over-interpret, this
difference. The direct term has a fitted ratio of
$-0.406$~nm~s$^{-2}$~mm$^{-1}$ and RMS and peak-to-peak amplitudes equal to
18.0\% and 18.1\% of the combined elastic term. Their diagnostic arithmetic sum
is $-2.672$~nm~s$^{-2}$~mm$^{-1}$, 0.45\% from the published ratio, but this is
not the primary comparison: the fixed target is the combined elastic
quantity and the source paper's observable convention cannot be redefined
post hoc. PREM/CE versus SPOTL/Gutenberg--Bullen Earth response and the 30-day
fit versus three-year detiding remain confounded in the available computation,
so no causal percentage is assigned to either source.

\section{Results}
Figure~\ref{fig:frequency} compares process timescales with the reviewed
instrument-curve support. M2 tide and the M2 internal-wave branch lie near
$2.24\times10^{-5}$~Hz. Storm-surge and eddy ranges are lower. The iGrav anchor
begins at $10^{-3}$~Hz and the reviewed AQG/FG5 plateaus at
$5\times10^{-4}$~Hz. Tsunami characteristic content
crosses these edges, while the Storegga transition lies mainly below them; neither
has 90\% of signal energy inside a complete reviewed band.

The resulting native-grid coverage matrix is retained as Supplementary
Figure~S1. All 806
long-period records have no numerically integrable common interval for any
reviewed curve. For several representations the source Nyquist frequency itself
lies below the lowest curve edge; other cases have fewer than two native
Fourier samples in the common interval. These conditions are not interpreted as
zero physical energy.
All 640 tsunami and Storegga records have some overlapping bins but fail the
90\% energy criterion. Consequently no production record is labelled detectable
or undetectable. This differs from a statement of zero SNR: the represented
source spectra and reviewed curve support do not provide an adequate common
interval for the comparison. In Supplementary Figure~S1, ``no band'' denotes
the original native-grid implementation condition of fewer than two positive
frequency samples, while ``partial'' means a resolved overlap covers less than
90\% of signal energy.

Figure~\ref{fig:requirements} separates dense-grid convergence from
record-window dependence. All six process medians converge within 5\% between
16- and 64-times finite-DTFT sampling, and all five regenerable analytic
families pass the 4- versus 8-times cadence gate. Exact-cycle tide windows give
median 90\% lower edges of $2.157$--$2.216\times10^{-5}$~Hz and 2--8-period
internal-wave windows give $1.817$--$2.101\times10^{-5}$~Hz; their
largest-two-window changes are 1.00\% and 5.20\%. The other branches are window
limited: the tested ranges are $1.407$--$2.793\times10^{-8}$~Hz for the eddy,
$7.837\times10^{-7}$--$3.488\times10^{-6}$~Hz for the single storm case,
$5.990$--$9.903\times10^{-5}$~Hz for tsunami packets and
$7.764\times10^{-6}$--$1.846\times10^{-5}$~Hz for landslide transitions. These
are finite-record scenario diagnostics. Every tested value remains below the
most permissive reviewed $5\times10^{-4}$~Hz edge, and dense boundary-aware
integration still yields zero 90\%-coverage passes among 1,446 records.

Direct radial-gravity amplitudes decay with spherical surface standoff
(Figure~\ref{fig:distance}). The Storegga named-event branch has the largest
primary-branch envelope, with median peak amplitude decreasing from about
$9.5\times10^{-9}$~m~s$^{-2}$ at 10~km to $1.0\times10^{-10}$~m~s$^{-2}$ at
1,000~km. The compensated tsunami envelope decreases from approximately
$3.1$--$6.5\times10^{-10}$ to $4.9$--$12.7\times10^{-12}$~m~s$^{-2}$ across the
same standoffs. The internal-wave dipole has an off-axis maximum near 100~km,
illustrating why amplitude need not decrease monotonically inside a compensated
source's near field.

wave-packet and slide-parameter envelopes. Every tsunami crest and trough pair
cancels signed surface mass to numerical tolerance, and every slide record
relocates rather than creates solid mass. Its bands are design extrema, not

A separate version-controlled structural-closure experiment maps the
$3.0\times10^{15}$~J Tohoku propagated-energy estimate (6\% uncertainty)
reported by Tang et al.~\cite{Tang2012} to the same idealized mass-balanced
Gaussian packet. Under the declared linear-long-wave convention
$E=\rho g\int\eta^2\,\mathrm{d}A$, the 300--400~km separation sensitivity gives
a central crest amplitude of 4.359~m and an energy-uncertainty range of
4.226--4.488~m. This branch is deliberately separate from the 1.0--1.8~m
deep-ocean amplitude-normalized branch; their difference measures
normalization/model-form sensitivity and is not combined as a probability
interval.

\section{Discussion}
The principal result is a boundary between classified, coverage-limited, and
physically suppressed signals. Compensated internal structures can decay much
faster than surface-load estimates, while long-period processes may be strong
but unclassifiable when empirical noise curves do not cover their frequency
band. An amplitude--noise intersection is not a sufficient detectability test:
observation duration and spectral energy coverage must be evaluated first.

The absence of a production SNR classification is therefore informative. It
identifies a data requirement---site and instrument noise curves extending into
the process band---rather than an invitation to extend short-term white-noise
anchors by assumption. Environmental Newtonian-noise calculations expressed as
gravitational-wave strain are likewise not interchangeable with gravimeter
self-noise~\cite{Brundu2022}.

The threshold and convergence analyses distinguish a robust common-support
conclusion from finite-record sensitivity. At every tested energy fraction and
record window, the process medians remain below $5\times10^{-4}$~Hz. Dense
frequency sampling and finer cadence do not change that conclusion. However,
$f_{\mathrm{low}}T$ remains nearly constant as the eddy, storm, tsunami and
landslide windows change, demonstrating that their exact edges are controlled
partly by record definition. Future field studies should therefore report a
record-window convergence analysis together with the low-frequency extent of
the measured site PSD, rather than adopt these four ranges as instrument-design
constants.

The review audit reconstructed all 1,446 signals using the source-reported AQG
and FG5 intervals and repeated the 50--95\% calculation with
rectangular and periodic Hann periodogram bin sums. Every record retains its
no-band or partial-band status. Median 90\% requirements remain below the lowest
reviewed edge for every process and convention. The largest convention effect
is the internal-wave rectangular periodogram result,
$2.20\times10^{-5}$~Hz, twice the fixed trapezoidal value but still a
factor of 23 below $5\times10^{-4}$~Hz. These checks establish robustness to the
numerical convention, not natural population uncertainty.

The subsequent convergence audit changes the quantitative interpretation but
not the primary classification result. Boundary-aware 64-times sampling finds
no 90\%-coverage pass among the 1,446 records. It resolves the former first-bin
quantization and confirms stable tide and internal-wave estimates, while
identifying eddy, storm-surge, tsunami and landslide edges as window limited.
Accordingly the native-grid P1-E008 values remain reproducible historical
outputs but are not used as universal measurement requirements.

The Helgoland calculation supplies an independent model-scale consistency
check, reproducing the published elastic gravity-to-height ratio within 15.6\%,
but it does not validate observation/model correlation. The reported
85~nm~s$^{-2}$ and $-34$~mm extrema are observations, not BSH model acceptance
targets. Reproducing the reported correlation and RMS reduction would require
authenticated iGrav047 data and a paper-equivalent processed 2022 GNSS series.

\section{Conclusions}
The reviewed public instrument references do not support a comprehensive
cross-process detectability assessment: no production record reaches the required
signal-energy coverage. This result does not imply absent physical signals or
universal non-detectability. Dense spectral and cadence convergence preserves
the zero-classification result, while record-window tests show that four of six
exact low-frequency edges are scenario-window dependent. The direct-gravity
suite quantifies how geometry, distance and mass compensation alter the source
signal and defines the additional temporal-support evidence a future field
study must measure and converge. Total terrestrial observables require
separately validated elastic/potential and height components and are not
inferred from the direct atlas alone.

\section{Data and code availability}
Code, compact experiment metadata, checksums, figure inputs and manuscript
sources are maintained in the public project repository. Bulk BSH NetCDF and

source queries, inventories and checksums. Restricted gravimeter data and large

\section{Limitations}
The production atlas contains one primary branch per process family, plus the
energy-normalized tsunami sensitivity. Remaining structural branches are
explicit scientific exclusions, not hidden omissions or zero-effect
assumptions. The eddy design cannot simultaneously hold peak SSH, radius, and
positive mass fixed across Gaussian and compact quadratic profiles; the
compensated eddy lacks an SSH-to-density-profile mapping. Continuous mode-1 and
free-surface internal-wave branches lack a boundary-conditioned vertical
profile and coupling coefficient. Mediterranean/Storfjorden slide evidence does
not jointly close amplitude and duration, and the generated-water-wave branch
lacks an event-specific generation/entrainment transfer model.

The reviewed instrument curves are literature references rather than complete
site-specific environmental noise models. The iGrav curve estimates quiet
self-noise, AQG and FG5 entries are short-term anchors, and none authorizes
low-frequency extrapolation. Thus the matrix establishes a coverage boundary,
not universal instrument performance. Haikou observation reproduction remains
author-restricted; this does not affect the six-process ensemble. A PEGS
simulator is unrelated to Paper~1. The repository release uses collective
project authorship. The journal revision identifies the six authors, their two
affiliations, and Fan Zhang as corresponding author; ORCIDs and the author-approved
CRediT statement are not inferred by the analysis repository.
